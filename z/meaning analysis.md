
In dealing with many abstract issues, often the first task is to clarify the relevant key terms or concepts. For example, to find out whether Asian values are incompatible with human rights, we have to explain what exactly "Asian values" and "human rights" mean.

Good communication skills involve being able to convey messages with the right meaning, and being able to understand the meaning of what has been said, or left unsaid.

## Literal Meaning & Conversational Implicature

The literal meaning of a statement should be distinguished from its **conversational implicature** - the information that is implicitly conveyed in a particular conversational context, distinct from the literal meaning of the statement.

For example, suppose we ask Lily whether she wants to go to the cinema and she replies, "I am very tired." Naturally we would infer that Lily does not want to go to the cinema.

---

When we want to distinguish between the literal meaning of two statements, one method is to think of situations where one statement is true, and the other one is false, or to think of the different implications that they might have. Apply this method to each of these pairs of sentences.

I shall try to come. (may or may not come)
I shall come. (come for sure)

Honesty is Tim's biggest virtue.
Loyalty is Tim's biggest virtue. 
(One may have to lie in order to protect the one is loyal to)

You may terminate the contract after 12 months by giving 2 months notice.
After 12 months, you can give 2 months notice and terminate the contract.
(In the first statement, your effective stay is 12 months. In second statement, your effective stay is 14 months)
